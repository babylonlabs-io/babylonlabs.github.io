# Liquidation risk

This page describes liquidation in the context of the **Aave v4 integration** — the first DeFi application built on TBV. Other applications that may register with the protocol in the future could have different liquidation mechanisms. What is constant across applications is the underlying TBV protocol layer (vault creation, redemption, fraud detection); see [What is TBV?](../start-here/what-is-tbv.mdx) → "Two layers" for the overview.

Liquidation is what happens when your debt outgrows the value of your collateral. This page is written from the depositor's side: when it triggers, what gets seized, what you keep, and how to avoid it. For the protocol-side mechanics, see [Aave v4 integration](../technical-details/aave-v4-integration.mdx).

## When liquidation can happen

A position becomes liquidatable when the health factor drops below 1.0. The health factor compares the risk-adjusted value of your collateral to the value of your debt:

```
Health Factor = (Total Collateral Value × Collateral Factor) / Total Debt Value
```

On the current Testnet, the collateral factor for vaultBTC is 78%. Two things move the health factor:

* **BTC price drops.** Your vaultBTC collateral loses value in USD terms while debt stays denominated in the borrowed token.
* **Interest accrues.** Your debt grows continuously under the Aave v4 interest rate model; the protocol additionally takes a slice of accrued interest as a fee.

Once the health factor crosses below 1.0, any Ethereum address can call the liquidation function. Liquidation is permissionless by design: anyone can execute it, and the protocol pays a bonus to whoever does. In practice, professional liquidator bots compete for these events; once a position falls below 1.0, a liquidation typically clears within the next block or two.

## What "liquidation" actually seizes

Two terms describe distinct things, and they apply at the same time:

* **Full-vault liquidation.** Each individual vault is seized whole. This is structural: a vault is backed by an indivisible Bitcoin UTXO, so the protocol cannot seize "half" of a vault. When a vault is seized, the whole vault moves.
* **Partial-position liquidation.** When your position contains multiple vaults, the protocol seizes only the subset of vaults needed to restore the health factor to its target. The rest stay in your position.

The protocol walks your vault list in order during liquidation and takes consecutive vaults from the front until their cumulative value covers the protocol's target seizure amount. The target seizure depends on the target health factor (THF) and your collateral and debt sizes.

If your position contains a single vault, partial-position is not possible: that one vault is your entire collateral, and seizing it discharges the whole position in one event. This is the **cliff effect**: a single small price move can take everything backing the position.

## The two-vault mitigation (regime-aware)

The portal recommends splitting your BTC into two vaults at peg-in: a **sacrificial vault** (sized to cover the protocol's expected seizure amount, placed first in the liquidation order) and a **protected vault** (whatever BTC remains, placed second).

The sacrificial vault's size depends on the target health factor (THF) and other protocol parameters. The portal computes the recommended split dynamically from the on-chain values. At current public-testnet parameters, the protocol's target seizure is approximately **62% of your total BTC** when a position becomes liquidatable — so the sacrificial vault must be sized to cover roughly two-thirds of your position. At these parameters, the sacrificial vault is the **larger** of the two and is placed first. See [Protocol parameters](../testnet-info/protocol-parameters.mdx) for the live numeric inputs.

**Note on ordering.** At lower THF values (1.05–1.22), the seized fraction is smaller (under 50%) and the sacrificial vault would be the smaller of the two. The "smaller-or-larger sacrificial" choice depends on THF; the portal handles this automatically. The principle is constant: the sacrificial vault is whichever size matches the expected seizure, and it always sits first in the order.

### Worked example with public-testnet parameters

Suppose you peg in **1 BTC** and split it as recommended. With the current public-testnet target seizure of approximately 62.5% and a small safety margin:

* Target seizure = 1 × 62.5% ≈ **0.625 BTC**
* Sacrificial vault = 0.625 × 1.05 (safety margin) ≈ **0.66 BTC** — placed first
* Protected vault = 1 − 0.66 ≈ **0.34 BTC** — placed second

If the position becomes liquidatable, the protocol walks your vaults in order. The sacrificial vault alone covers the target seizure, so only that one vault is seized. The protected vault stays in your position.

Without the split, the same liquidation seizes the full single vault. That is the cliff effect.

You can reorder vaults after creation. If price action changes which vault is the right sacrificial buffer, reorder it before the position becomes unhealthy. See [Create a vault](./create-a-vault.mdx) for the two-vault setup at peg-in.

## Fairness payment: you do not lose the excess

Because vaults are indivisible, the seized vault almost always carries slightly more BTC than a perfectly divisible partial liquidation would have taken. The **fairness mechanism** returns the excess to you.

The protocol computes the over-seizure surplus and applies one of two compensations, depending on whether you have remaining debt after the liquidation:

* **Fairness debt repay** — if the surplus is smaller than your remaining debt, the surplus reduces your remaining debt. No WBTC is paid out; the debt position simply shrinks.
* **Fairness payment** — if the surplus exceeds your remaining debt (or your debt is fully repaid by the seizure), the leftover surplus is paid to you in WBTC.

The fairness payment is denominated in WBTC rather than native BTC because the seized vault's BTC has already moved to the liquidator on Bitcoin — the protocol cannot conjure new BTC mid-liquidation. WBTC is the closest BTC-pegged asset available on Ethereum and trades very close to BTC parity (typically around 0.9975 BTC per WBTC due to wrapping friction). You can later redeem WBTC for native BTC through any WBTC custodian if you want to consolidate back to Bitcoin.

Net effect: after a single-vault overshoot, your remaining position sits where a perfectly divisible partial liquidation would have left it. You keep the excess BTC value (as WBTC), and your remaining debt is reduced where applicable.

If the WBTC transfer to your address fails (for example, the address is blocked by the token contract), the payment is redirected to the protocol's `applicationRecipient` address.

## What the liquidator earns

The protocol pays the liquidator a **liquidation bonus**: collateral worth more than the debt repaid. The bonus is capped at `maxLiquidationBonus` = 10% on Testnet. The bonus scales with how far below 1.0 the health factor sits.

* **Health factor just below 1.0:** a smaller bonus. Recently unhealthy positions are not urgent.
* **Health factor at or below the max-bonus threshold:** the maximum 10% bonus applies. The protocol pays more to make sure deeply underwater positions clear quickly.
* **Health factor at 1.0 or above:** liquidation is impossible. No bonus exists.

From the depositor's side, the bonus is the cost the protocol pays out of your collateral to compensate the liquidator. The fairness payment then returns any leftover to you.

There is no separate per-vault liquidation fee on BTC vault collateral — vaults are indivisible UTXOs, so an Aave-style liquidation fee on the BTC side is structurally impossible. Depending on which path the liquidator uses, an additional fee may apply:

* **Direct-redemption path** (registered arbitrageur with a Bitcoin redeem key): a 5% liquidation redemption fee applies (`liquidationRedemptionFeeBps` = 500 BPS), deducted from your seized-vault value.
* **LLP path (BTCVaultSwap)**: a 30% LLP commission applies on the arbitrageur's margin (`discountCommissionBps`), plus a 1.5% sell discount that the LLP takes on the WBTC paid to the liquidator. Both reduce the value available for your fairness payment.

Specific bonus parameters are listed on the [Protocol parameters](../testnet-info/protocol-parameters.mdx) page.

## What is left after liquidation

After the liquidation transaction settles:

* Seized vaults move to `Redeemed` status. Their underlying BTC is the liquidator's, who can either redeem on Bitcoin directly (registered arbitrageurs) or sell the vault through the BTCVaultSwap LLP. See [Aave v4 integration](../technical-details/aave-v4-integration.mdx).
* Unseized vaults stay in your position as `InUse` collateral. The position remains open as long as some debt and some collateral remain.
* Your debt is reduced by the liquidator's repayment, plus the fairness debt repay if applicable.
* The fairness payment, if any, lands in your Ethereum address as WBTC.
* Your remaining health factor is at or above target.

In the cliff case (single-vault position), the seized vault is your whole collateral. The position ends. The fairness payment still applies, so any surplus value still returns to you.

## Health factor zones

| Health factor | What it means | What to do |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Above 1.5 | Comfortable margin | Monitor during BTC volatility |
| 1.2 to 1.5 | Approaching risk | Consider repaying some debt or adding collateral |
| 1.0 to 1.2 | High risk | Repay or add collateral immediately |
| Below 1.0 | Liquidatable | Position can be liquidated at any moment |

The zones above 1.0 are guidance, not contract values. The only on-chain threshold is the 1.0 boundary.

## How to avoid liquidation

* **Borrow conservatively.** Stay well below the maximum your collateral allows. A health factor above 1.5 leaves headroom for normal BTC volatility.
* **Use the two-vault split.** It is the most effective protection against the cliff effect, and the portal recommends it by default at peg-in. The portal also sizes the split for current parameters; don't manually undersize the sacrificial vault.
* **Trust the portal's ordering.** The recommended order (sacrificial first) is what the protocol walks during liquidation. Reordering matters; review your order when parameters change.
* **Monitor BTC price and health factor.** Call `getUserAccountData()` on the Babylon Core Spoke to read the live health factor.
* **Repay early when the health factor declines.** Partial repayments restore the health factor without closing the position. See [Borrow & repay](./borrow-and-repay.mdx).
* **Add collateral.** Add more vaults to the position up to the current public-testnet caps. Each added vault raises collateral value and the health factor; you may need to reorder afterwards. See [Protocol parameters](../testnet-info/protocol-parameters.mdx) for the live limits.
* **Withdraw redundant vaults.** If a vault is not needed for collateral, withdrawing it does not reduce safety because the health factor check applies to the post-withdrawal position. See [Withdraw & redeem](./withdraw-and-redeem.mdx).

## What is structurally bounded

A few protections sit at the contract level, not the user level:

* **You never owe more than your collateral covers.** Liquidation discharges debt against the seized vault. Excess collateral is paid back to you as the fairness payment. The protocol does not pursue residual debt against you outside the position.
* **A healthy position cannot be liquidated.** The 1.0 threshold is enforced on-chain at the moment of liquidation.
* **No rehypothecation of the underlying BTC.** The bitcoin sitting in a vault's Taproot output is not lent or repurposed by any party during the life of the vault. See [Safety & trust assumptions](../start-here/safety-and-trust-assumptions.mdx).
