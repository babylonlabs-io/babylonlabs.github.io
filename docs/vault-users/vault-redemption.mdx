# Vault Redemption

Vault redemption (also called **peg-out**) is the process of unlocking your
Bitcoin on the Bitcoin network after you are done using it as collateral. There
are three paths to getting your BTC back, depending on the situation.

## Redemption Paths

### 1. Normal Redemption (You Repay Your Loan)

This is the standard path. When you are ready to get your Bitcoin back:

1. **Repay your loan.** Pay back any outstanding debt on Aave v4.
2. **Withdraw your vault.** Remove the vault from your lending position. It
   transitions from InUse to Available.
3. **Initiate redemption.** Call the redemption function through the web portal
   or CLI. The vault transitions to **Redeemed** status and any associated
   vaultBTC tokens are burned.

From here, the vault provider handles the Bitcoin-side claim process
automatically. You receive your BTC at the Bitcoin address you specified during
vault creation.

### 2. Self-Claim (WOTS Key Fallback)

If the vault provider becomes unresponsive after you have initiated redemption,
you are not stuck. As a depositor, you hold WOTS key material from vault
creation that enables you to initiate a claim on Bitcoin directly. This
self-claim path ensures you can always recover your BTC, even if your vault
provider goes offline.

### 3. Liquidation

If your lending position's health factor drops below 1.0, your position can be
liquidated by any participant on the network:

1. A liquidator repays your debt and seizes your vaults.
2. The vaults transition to **Redeemed** status.
3. The liquidator (or a vault keeper acting as a Liquidation Liquidity Provider)
   claims the underlying Bitcoin through the same proof-and-claim mechanism.

During liquidation, you receive a **fairness payment** -- a portion of value
returned to you even though your position was liquidated. The liquidator pays
a fee to the protocol.

## Activate-and-Redeem Shortcut

If you have a vault in **Active** status that you have not yet used as
collateral, you can skip the lending step entirely and go straight to
redemption. This is useful if you created a vault but changed your mind before
borrowing anything.

## What Happens Behind the Scenes

Regardless of which path triggers the redemption, the same trustless mechanism
ensures your Bitcoin is released correctly.

### ZK Proof Generation

When a vault is redeemed and vaultBTC is burned on Ethereum, the protocol needs
to prove to the Bitcoin network that this actually happened. Since Bitcoin cannot
natively verify Ethereum state, a zero-knowledge proof is generated:

1. **Beacon finality proof** -- Confirms that a specific Ethereum block has been
   finalized.
2. **Execution finality proof** -- Links the finalized block to the execution
   layer containing the burn transaction.
3. **Receipt inclusion proof** -- Proves the specific burn event exists in a
   transaction receipt within that block.
4. **Compressed Groth16 proof** -- Aggregates and compresses the above into a
   compact 128-byte proof plus 32 bytes of public input.

This entire process is handled by the vault provider's infrastructure using
SP1 CUDA provers and typically takes **5-10 minutes**. You do not need to do
anything during this step.

### Claim and Payout on Bitcoin

Once the proof is ready, the Bitcoin-side claim process begins:

1. **Claim transaction.** The authorized redeemer broadcasts a claim transaction
   on Bitcoin, spending from the vault's Taproot output. This includes a
   commitment to the ZK proof.

2. **Challenge period.** A timelock window opens during which universal
   challengers can verify the proof. If everything is valid (the expected case),
   no challenge is raised.

3. **Payout transaction.** After the challenge period passes without dispute,
   the payout transaction is broadcast, releasing your BTC to the designated
   Bitcoin address.

This is the **optimistic path** -- in a properly functioning system, the vast
majority of redemptions follow it without any challenges.

### If a Challenge Is Raised

In the rare case that a universal challenger detects an invalid proof:

1. The challenger broadcasts a challenge transaction during the timelock window.
2. The claimer responds by posting their proof on-chain via ChallengeAssert
   transactions, which are verified using BitVM3 garbled circuits and WOTS
   signatures.
3. **If the proof is valid:** The payout proceeds and the challenger loses their
   bond.
4. **If the proof is invalid:** The challenger extracts the claimer's secret,
   proving fraud. The claimer loses their bond and your BTC is protected.

Challenges are a security mechanism, not a routine operation. They serve as a
deterrent against fraud.

## Bitcoin Transaction Costs

The normal redemption path (no challenge) involves a small number of Bitcoin
transactions with a total cost of approximately **2,000-3,000 satoshis** in
miner fees at typical fee rates. These costs are paid by the claimer (vault
provider or vault keeper), not by you.

If a challenge occurs, additional transactions increase costs, but this is
expected to be rare.

## Redemption Summary

| Path | Trigger | Who Claims on Bitcoin | BTC Goes To |
|------|---------|----------------------|-------------|
| Normal redemption | Loan repaid, vault withdrawn | Vault provider | Your BTC address |
| Self-claim | Vault provider unresponsive | You (via WOTS key) | Your BTC address |
| Liquidation (direct) | Position liquidated | Liquidator | Liquidator's BTC address |
| Liquidation (via settlement) | Liquidator sells to vault keeper | Vault keeper (LLP) | Vault keeper's BTC address |

In all cases, the ZK proof generation and challenge mechanism ensure that
Bitcoin is only released when the correct conditions have been met on Ethereum
-- without requiring trust in any single intermediary.
