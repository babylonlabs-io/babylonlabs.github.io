# Vault Redemption

Vault redemption (also called **peg-out**) is the process of unlocking your
Bitcoin on the Bitcoin network after you are done using it as collateral. There
are two paths to redemption: **depositor redemption** (the normal case, after
repaying your loan) and **liquidation redemption** (when a position is liquidated
and the vault is seized).

In both cases, the fundamental challenge is the same: proving to the Bitcoin
network that the correct events happened on Ethereum — without Bitcoin having
native smart contract capabilities.

## Redemption Triggers

A vault can be redeemed when:

1. **You withdraw it from your lending position** after repaying all debt. The
   vault transitions from InUse to Available, and you can then initiate a
   depositor redemption.
2. **Your position is liquidated.** A liquidator repays your debt, seizes your
   vaults, and the vault transitions directly to Redeemed. The liquidator (or a
   subsequent arbitrageur) can then claim the underlying Bitcoin.

In both cases, the on-chain contract emits a `VaultRedeemable` event containing
the vault ID, the BTC amount, the public keys of authorized redeemers, and the
depositor's address. This event is the starting signal for the off-chain proof
generation and Bitcoin claim process.

## Depositor Redemption (Normal Path)

When you have repaid all debt and withdrawn your vault from the lending position:

1. You call `depositorRedeem(vaultId)` on the AaveIntegrationController.
2. The contract validates that you are the original depositor and the vault is in
   Available status.
3. The vault status transitions to **Redeemed** (terminal state).
4. The contract burns any associated vaultBTC tokens.
5. A `VaultRedeemable` event is emitted with the vault provider's BTC public key
   as the authorized redeemer.

The vault provider, upon detecting this event, initiates the Bitcoin-side claim
process.

## Liquidation Redemption

When your position is liquidated:

1. A liquidator calls `liquidateCorePosition()` and repays your debt.
2. All vaults in the position are seized — ownership transfers from you to the
   liquidator.
3. Vaults transition to Redeemed status.
4. `VaultRedeemable` events are emitted, but this time the authorized redeemer is
   the liquidator (not the vault provider).

The liquidator can then either claim the BTC directly or sell the vault through
the liquidation settlement process to an arbitrageur, who will handle the Bitcoin
claim.

## The ZK Proof Generation Process

The key innovation of the protocol is how Bitcoin learns about events that
happened on Ethereum. Since Bitcoin cannot natively verify Ethereum state, the
protocol uses zero-knowledge proofs generated off-chain and verified on Bitcoin
using BitVM3 garbled circuits.

### 4-Stage Aggregated Burn Event Proof

When a vault is redeemed and vaultBTC is burned on Ethereum, a proof must be
generated that this burn actually occurred. This proof has four stages:

**Stage 1: Beacon Finality Proof.** Proves that a specific Ethereum beacon chain
block has been finalized by the consensus layer. This establishes that the
Ethereum state at that block height is final and cannot be reverted.

**Stage 2: Execution Finality Proof.** Links the beacon chain block to the
execution layer, proving that a specific execution block (containing the burn
transaction) is part of the finalized chain.

**Stage 3: Receipt Inclusion Proof.** Proves that the specific `BurnExecuted`
event log exists in a transaction receipt within the finalized block. This is the
actual proof that vaultBTC was burned for a specific vault.

**Stage 4: Compressed Groth16 Proof.** The first three proofs are aggregated and
compressed into a single Groth16 proof — just 128 bytes of proof data plus 32
bytes of public input. This compact proof is what gets verified on the Bitcoin
network.

The entire proof generation pipeline uses SP1 CUDA provers and typically takes
5-10 minutes per vault on GPU hardware. This is handled by the vault provider's
infrastructure.

## Claim and Challenge Periods

Once the proof is generated, the Bitcoin-side claim process begins. This uses a
carefully designed sequence of transactions with timelocks and challenge
opportunities.

### The Claim Process

1. **Claim Transaction.** The authorized redeemer (vault provider or liquidator)
   broadcasts a claim transaction on Bitcoin, spending from the vault's Taproot
   output. This transaction includes a commitment to the ZK proof.

2. **Payout (Optimistic Path).** If no challenge is raised within the timelock
   period, the payout transaction is broadcast, releasing the BTC to the
   depositor's Bitcoin address. This is the happy path — in a properly functioning
   system, most redemptions follow this path.

3. **Challenge Period.** During the timelock window, any universal challenger can
   challenge the claim by broadcasting a challenge transaction. Challenges are
   appropriate when a claim is made with an invalid proof.

### Challenge Resolution

If a challenge is raised:

1. **Assert Transaction.** The claimer broadcasts an assert transaction, providing
   their ZK proof on-chain.

2. **Verification via BitVM3.** The proof is verified using BitVM3 garbled
   circuits. These circuits were pre-exchanged between participants during vault
   creation (Stage 2 of the peg-in process).

3. **Outcomes:**
   - **Valid proof:** The claimer's assertion holds. The payout transaction
     proceeds, releasing BTC to the correct recipient. The challenger loses their
     bond.
   - **Invalid proof:** The challenger can extract the claimer's secret from the
     garbled circuit off-chain, proving fraud. The challenger claims the claimer's
     bond, and the vault's BTC is protected.

### Timelock Parameters

The protocol uses Bitcoin block-based timelocks for the claim and challenge
periods:

- **t1 (Claimer Proof Time):** Time window for the claimer to post their proof
  after a claim.
- **t2 (Challenger Challenge Time):** Time window for challengers to submit a
  challenge after seeing a claim.
- **t3 (Claimer Disprove Time):** Time window for the claimer to disprove a
  challenge.

These parameters are set as protocol-level configuration (offchain parameters)
and apply to all vaults. They are versioned, so changes to timelock values only
affect newly created vaults — existing vaults retain the parameters from when
they were created.

## Bitcoin Transaction Costs

The normal redemption path (no challenge) requires three Bitcoin transactions:

- **Claim transaction** — broadcast by the claimer
- **Proof transaction** — posts the ZK proof commitment
- **Payout transaction** — releases BTC to the depositor

Total cost for the normal path is approximately 2,000-3,000 satoshis in Bitcoin
network fees at typical fee rates.

If a challenge occurs, additional transactions are needed (ChallengeAssertX,
ChallengeAssertY), which increases costs significantly. However, challenges are
expected to be rare in a properly functioning system — they serve as a deterrent
against fraud rather than a routine operation.

## Redemption Summary

| Path | Trigger | Authorized Redeemer | BTC Goes To |
|------|---------|-------------------|-------------|
| Depositor Redemption | Loan repaid, vault withdrawn | Vault Provider | Depositor's BTC address |
| Liquidation (Direct) | Position liquidated | Liquidator | Liquidator's BTC address |
| Liquidation (via LLP Settlement) | Liquidator sells to LLP | LLP (vault keeper) | LLP's BTC address |

In all cases, the ZK proof generation and challenge mechanism ensures that
Bitcoin is only released when the correct Ethereum-side conditions have been
met — without requiring any trust in intermediaries.
