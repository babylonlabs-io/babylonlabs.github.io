# Vault Creation

Creating a vault is the process of locking Bitcoin on the Bitcoin network and
making it available as collateral on Ethereum. This is called a **peg-in**.

Before any Bitcoin moves, the protocol coordinates multiple participants to
construct a set of pre-signed Bitcoin transactions that encode every possible
outcome -- repayment, liquidation, and fraud challenge. This ensures your BTC
is protected from the start.

You can perform vault creation through the **web portal** or the **CLI**.

## The User Experience

At a high level, creating a vault involves three phases:

### Phase 1: Initiate

You start by generating a secret, submitting a vault creation request on
Ethereum, and broadcasting a Bitcoin transaction. Specifically:

- You generate a random **secret** and compute its hash (the **hashlock**).
  This secret is the key to activating your vault later.
- You submit a peg-in request to the **BTCVaultRegistry** contract on Ethereum,
  including your Bitcoin public key, the deposit amount, the hashlock, your
  chosen vault provider, and a BIP-322 proof of key ownership.
- A small **peg-in fee** (in ETH) is required to prevent spam.
- You sign and broadcast a **Pre-PegIn transaction** on Bitcoin. This locks
  your BTC in an HTLC (Hash Time-Lock Contract) output tied to your hashlock.
  The HTLC ensures your BTC can only be spent if all parties sign AND the
  secret is revealed -- or refunded to you after a timeout.

The web portal handles the secret generation, hashlock computation, and
Pre-PegIn construction automatically.

### Phase 2: Setup (Automatic)

This phase happens automatically in the background -- you do not need to take
any action beyond signing when prompted.

1. **Depositor authentication.** Your wallet authenticates to the vault
   provider using an auth anchor (a signed challenge proving you control
   the Bitcoin key). If unavailable, a BIP-322 signature is used as fallback.
   This establishes a secure session for the remaining setup steps.

2. **Bitcoin confirmations.** The protocol waits for your Pre-PegIn
   transaction to reach 6 confirmations on Bitcoin (~60 minutes). This is the
   longest step and runs concurrently with the steps below.

3. **Transaction graph construction.** The vault provider and vault keepers
   create a complete set of pre-signed Bitcoin transactions covering every
   possible vault outcome. These participants coordinate via gRPC and
   cross-sign each other's transaction sets.

4. **WOTS keypair setup.** Participants generate Winternitz One-Time Signature
   (WOTS) keypairs for the BitVM3 verification system. This involves setting
   up 6 garbled circuit (GC) instances per counterparty relationship, which
   enable trustless proof verification on Bitcoin later.

5. **PegIn input signing.** All parties (vault provider, vault keepers,
   universal challengers) sign the PegIn input -- the spend of your Pre-PegIn
   HTLC output. These signatures are posted on-chain for data availability.

6. **Payout signing.** You sign the payout transactions, authorizing the
   conditions under which your BTC can be released. The web portal or CLI
   handles this signing step for you.

7. **Acknowledgment collection.** Each participant submits an on-chain
   acknowledgment (ACK) confirming they hold a complete set of pre-signed
   transactions and that the Pre-PegIn has 6 confirmations. Once all ACKs
   are collected, the vault status moves to **Verified**.

### Phase 3: Activate

With the vault verified and all signatures in place:

1. **Reveal your secret.** You call `activateVaultWithSecret` on Ethereum,
   revealing the secret that matches the hashlock from Phase 1. Only you can
   do this, since only you know the secret. The vault status transitions to
   **Active**.

2. **PegIn broadcast.** The vault provider and vault keepers detect the
   revealed secret on Ethereum, construct the full PegIn transaction witness
   (combining all party signatures with your secret), and broadcast the PegIn
   transaction to Bitcoin. This moves your BTC from the Pre-PegIn HTLC into
   the vault's Taproot script address.

This activation mechanism (HTLC atomic swap) eliminates the need for an
on-chain Bitcoin light client and completes in minutes once the secret is
revealed.

## After Activation

Once active, your vault can be:

- **Used as collateral** on Aave v4 to borrow stablecoins and other assets.
- **Combined with other vaults** in a single lending position.
- **Left idle** until you are ready to use it -- an active vault does not need
  to be used immediately.

Your Bitcoin remains locked on the Bitcoin network throughout. Once activated,
the vault can be deposited into the Aave integration on Ethereum and used as
collateral there.

## Timeouts and Refunds

If the peg-in process is not completed within the activation timeout (a protocol
parameter measured in Ethereum blocks), the vault expires. This prevents
indefinite resource locking and protects all participants from being committed
to stale transaction data.

If the vault expires before your Bitcoin transaction is broadcast, no BTC is at
risk since it was never sent. If the vault expires after you broadcast but before
activation, the pre-signed transaction graph includes a refund path that returns
your BTC.

## Timeline Summary

| Phase | What Happens | Approximate Duration |
|-------|-------------|----------|
| Phase 1: Initiate | Submit request on Ethereum + broadcast Pre-PegIn on Bitcoin | ~1-2 minutes |
| Phase 2: Setup | Auth, BTC confirmations, tx graphs, WOTS, signing, ACKs | ~60 minutes (dominated by 6 BTC confirmations) |
| Phase 3: Activate | Reveal secret, PegIn broadcast | ~1-2 minutes |
| **Total** | **End-to-end** | **~1 hour** |
